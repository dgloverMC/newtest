Abstract

This document provides a standard for implementation of virtualized x86 server infrastructure within Daimler AG data centers. Typical technologies needed for such infrastructure, as hypervisors, storage, backup/restore and network are outlined. Core part is the enumeration of threats against the availability of such infrastructures. Technical countermeasures facing these risks are defined. The document closes with a definition of CG IS Classes that may be used as implementation templates.

  1. Preface

This document aims to create a commonly applicable framework for virtualized infrastructures for any Daimler location. The main aspects are to achieve maximum availability at the best possible costs.

The architecture framework enhances the global standard documents of Daimler AG for virtualized server infrastructures. Relevant global documents are:

  • Global LAN Standard (GLS)

  • GLS Security Domains

  • Documents concerning data center infrastructures

  1. Background

In 2011, the Score2x (System Consolidation of RISC Environment – transfer to x86) project was originally intended to consolidate the IT infrastructure of Daimler AG and to provide a template for Continuity Critical systems, i.e. systems that need to be continually available to maintain production. Therefore, the Score2x platform is based on homogeneous, virtualized x86 components.

This guideline represents an update for the Score2x architecture based on technical innovations and on experience gained in recent years. The document was prepared by the Daimler Competence Group Integrated Systems (CG IS) who has extended the scope beyond the original Score2x project, thus naming it “CG IS Architecture Framework”.

The CG IS Architecture Framework is based on virtualization of x86 architectures and corresponding operating systems like Microsoft Windows, Red Hat Enterprise Linux (RHEL) or Suse Linux Enterprise Server (SLES). Thus, applications not able to run on top of these systems, like “Produktions Leit und Steuersystem (PLUS)”, which runs on a HP Nonstop System, or “Sicalis Automotive Suite”, which runs on HP-UX, are not within the scope of this framework.

The guideline provides a sound, future proof basis for standardized IT infrastructure within Daimler AG. The basic framework includes compute, network and storage architecture as well as high availability and disaster recovery. The framework could be used to support a certification process for infrastructure housing critical applications.

The architecture defined herein is augmented by additional documents in order to provide a shopping cart for such infrastructure and a practical guidance for implementing and configuring such integrated systems. Besides, local personnel may obtain shared services from a global support team, which supports implementation and operation, leading to synergies and cost savings.

  1. Motivation for Architecture Framework

2.2.1. Advantages of Virtualization

The tremendous success of virtualization technology results from various aspects. Primarily, workloads may be deployed without the need for dedicated hardware. Instead, virtual machines on hosts are set up. Complemented by integrated load balancing mechanisms, this results in a very efficient utilization of the existing infrastructure and in fast rollout processes for new applications. The optimization of necessary hardware also reduces data center footprint, energy consumption, and investment costs.

Furthermore, complex scale-up processes for individual workloads are replaced by scale-out with standardized hardware components. Instead of placing additional memory, processors or disks into existing physical servers, new physical servers with uniform configuration are added. This scale-out approach helps to reduce investment and maintenance costs by deploying mainstream hardware and reducing vendor dependence.

Features like live migration of virtual machines and storage increase the uptime of applications. Additionally, high availability features, fault tolerance mechanisms, and distributed resource scheduling improve business continuity and reliability of the IT infrastructure.

Thus, virtualized IT infrastructures guarantee reliable transfer of workloads and applications between data centers. This enables Daimler locations to be disaster-proof as several automated processes integrated in virtualization environments ensure quick failure recovery, even recovery of an entire data center.

2.2.2. Advantages of Standardization

Daimler AG will benefit tremendously from the standardization specified in the CG IS Architecture Framework. In particular, a wide distribution and use of the framework amongst Daimler locations will increase the benefits of standardization. Key aspects are:

Financial: Due to standardized equipment, volume-based contracts can be negotiated with vendors. Additionally, the purchase process is pre-determined and hence more effective.

Automation: Operating systems may be automatically implemented on standardized hardware. Prior to rollout, a central instance may preconfigure, test and certify infrastructure and operating systems as well as applications.

Support and documentation: Manuals, instructions, best practice documents and guidelines are provided by a central instance. Hence, actuality and availability is guaranteed and the location of the documents is well-known. Local IT staff may request support from central specialists to optimize problem management and troubleshooting. Therefore, less local resources need to be provided. Similarly, hardware and driver interoperability may be centrally tested and central release management can be made available.

Common solutions by central processes: A common framework allows the development and improvement of applications on well-known platforms. Central provisioning of processes simplifies workflows.

Security: CG IS Architecture Framework systems may be easily hardened according to Daimler policies. Amongst others, rollout of security hotfixes, updates of anti-virus software, and group policy integration are simplified for standardized systems.

Vendor support: Warranties and standard support contracts with better conditions may be negotiated if high numbers of standardized systems are concerned. Customer support with high quality and priority is also guaranteed.

Best practice solutions are thoroughly chosen for the CG IS Architecture Framework. To maximize the positive effects, a Daimler datacenter should implement components, software, and mechanisms released by CG IS.

  1. Motivation for Modular Architecture

In previous versions, the Architecture Framework uses a class-based approach, i.e. representative stack architectures meeting typical requirements or virtualized x86 environments are specified (Mode 1 IT). This approach does no longer meet the requirements and conditions of future applications and needs to specify an architecture logic that provides flexibility and modularity:

  • By now, Daimler uses applications that provide high availability due to its application architecture (so called Mode 2 IT). Thus, specific mechanisms on the infrastructure level specified in this Architecture Framework can be dispensed with.

  • On the other hand, a modular architecture for infrastructures allows the implementation of specific measures that addresses the typical threats to availability of an individual use case. Such measures can be implemented using appropriate technologies without the need for all measures of a higher infrastructure class.

The modular architecture framework defines typical risks and specifies corresponding measures that minimizes the threats resulting from the risks.

  1. Definition of Terms

The following specific terms are used throughout the document:

Backup: Copying data from primary storage media to secondary media. This secondary media is independent of primary storage media and of different type. The backup tool must not be included in the primary storage vendor toolchain and needs to be approved by the VCTs

Compute capacity includes CPU and memory resources.

Compute cluster: Multiple hosts with common management providing load balancing and redundancy.

Continuous Data Protection (CDP) Creation of recovery points with high frequency to allow RPOs of 1 minute or less. Data corruption or accidental deletion of data may be mitigated by rolling back to those older states.

Data Availability: Availability of data from applications’ perspective. RPO and RTO must be chosen according to application requirements on data availability.

Data center (DC): One or more rooms that house centrally used IT-Systems.

Data center (sister) site: Typically, a data center resides on a site. A sister site is a second site housing a redundant data center. A sister site is not necessarily located on the same premises.

Data Protection: Creation of recovery points on the primary storage media. Data corruption or accidental deletion of data may be mitigated by rolling back to those older states.

Development and Integration (Dev/Int): Environments that are used to develop applications and integrate them into production.

Hardware stack: An IT system that provides one or several physical servers including compute capacity and network connectivity as well as storage capacity.

Host: A physical server (used as a synonym) that provides resources for several virtual machines.

Infrastructure: As shown in Figure 1 : Differentiation between infrastructure and software in the CG IS Architecture framework, the term infrastructure generally includes storage, compute and networking hardware as well as the hypervisor.

Local Area Network (LAN): Network connecting clients and servers for client-server as well as server-server communication. Typically, the LAN covers the entire campus and bases on a hierarchical network design (see GLS).

Location: Daimler Group premises according to Global LAN Standard (GLS). The GLS distinguishes three categories of locations: Category A (large locations), Category B (medium locations) and Category C (small locations).

Productive applications/systems are supplying services for end users. Amongst others this includes manufacturing processes as well as customer services like Daimler web services.

RAMIS: Risks And Measures for Integrated Systems is a guided evaluation based on Microsoft Excel that makes covered/uncovered risks transparent for existing stacks. RAMIS additionally supports new implementations by suggesting proper measures to enhance availability.

Recovery Point: Point in time at which a consistent set of data can be restored.

Recovery Point Objective (RPO): Interval of time that might pass before data loss becomes critical, i.e. maximum time difference between Recovery Point and incident that can be tolerated.

Recovery Time Objective (RTO) Maximum duration of time during which a business process must be restored after a failure. Hence, RTO comprises the time needed for trouble shooting and repair as well as the time to start up the entire application infrastructure after successful repair.

Software: All layers above the hypervisor like operating system, middleware and application are summarized in the term software.

Storage cluster: Multiple storage units with common management providing redundancy.

Storage network: Network connecting storage units and compute nodes within a data center. The storage network is realized by means of Fiber Channel or Ethernet.

Storage unit: A physical component providing resources for data storage including storage controller, power supply and fan units.

  1. Predefined Infrastructure Solutions

The CG IS portfolio defines multiple infrastructure solutions that are applicable for all Daimler locations (see Chapter Threat Scenarios).

Solutions with similar service offerings are bundled into a solution group. Depending on the solution group the service offerings may vary as well as the associated documentation. So far four solution groups have been defined:

  • Custom Stack (Original Score2x) specifies schematics for hardware stacks based on a broad variety of the VCTs shopping cart items. Such stacks are supported by CG IS Custom Built Releases (CBR). More information can be found in the Daimler Social Intranet, Group Integrated Systems.

  • Type is a hardware stack predefined by CG IS. A specific parts list is published on the Daimler Social Intranet in the Group Integrated Systems. For every type, a type owner is assigned to handle the hardware and software architecture as well as the problem management. If a hardware stack is built according to the type specification, the stack operator will benefit from the centralized release planning offered by CG IS. Compatibility and interoperability of the integrated components is confirmed by CG IS for predefined solutions (except solution group custom stack). Therefore, Daimler locations may implement such hardware stacks without individual testing or proof of concept.

  • The solution group Converged Infrastructure (CI) covers manufacturer-specific hardware stacks including Compute, LAN, SAN and physical storage that are pre-build/configured by a manufacturer. The compute, storage and network components can be sized individually within certain ranges. The local data center operator of a Converged Infrastructure will get support by CG IS and the manufacturer. Amongst others, this includes release management for the hardware and problem management support.

  • The solution group Hyper-Converged Infrastructure (HCI) consists of hardware stack configurations that are pre-defined/configured by a manufacturer and allow limited choice of individual components. The manufacturer provides a centralized, simplified management solution and a pre-defined virtualization environment. Storage is based on virtualization of host-centric direct attached storage. The local data center operator will get VCT and Vendor support. Amongst others, this includes release management for the hardware integrated with the hypervisor and problem management support.

  1. Definition of Protection Requirements for Applications

Use of a modular architecture for stacks bases on an analysis of the protection requirements of the applications to be hosted. With this analysis, the main protection objectives Confidentiality, Integrity, and Availability (CIA) are determined for any application.

  1. Figure 3 : Main protection objectives of information security

This document mainly addresses the protection objective Availability.

Protection requirements of an application result from the potential damage that a malfunction or outage of the application and the corresponding data causes for the business processes. Metrics for protection requirements, especially availability, may be Recovery Time Objective (RTO, tolerable outage period) and Recovery Point Objective (RPO, tolerable period of data loss).

Protection requirements of stacks (and underlying infrastructure) are derived from the protection requirements of the corresponding applications. For example, a virtualization server and the respective data center must meet the same requirements as the hosted applications.

  1. Application Criticality

Applications used at any Daimler location are classified being either Business Enabling (BE) or Continuity Critical (CC). The implementer of an application relies on its classification which has been determined by the responsible Daimler instance.

Continuity Critical applications are defined by the highly negative impact on productive capacities in case of failures: The outage of a CC application causes considerable impact on important productive services. If failures have comparatively low impact, those applications are classified as BE. Examples of CC applications are SAP Enterprise-Resource-Planning and Process Integration (AM Supply), the Manufacturing Reporting System (MRS) or Flash and Diagnosis (IS-Tester).

Applications of Daimler AG are documented in the tool “Planning IT” with category BE or CC. A CG IS Architecture Framework system certified for CC workloads ensures a reliable infrastructure, but does not consider any specific prerequisite or specific operational process requested for CC applications.

  1. Data Availability

Data availability comprises several measures that are addressed by different technologies:

  • Storage availability. In other words, the storage infrastructure that accommodates the data must be up and running and connected to the application that requires the data.

  • (Continuous) Data protection: Is implemented to create Recovery Points for rollback after data corruption. Replica of data together with journals of transactions are used to rollback to previous states. Data protection allows RPOs with a minimum of 5 minutes. Continuous data protection guarantees a maximum RPO of 1 minute. With some technologies of CDP RPO approaches almost zero.

  • Data consistency: Also referred to as data integrity (in this case consistency refers to the correctness of data). To obtain data consistency all the elements of a dataset on a storage system are grouped (sometime referred to as “consistency group”). At a specific point in time all data of a consistency group that is being manipulated in volatile storage (e.g. cache, see Figure 4 : a) Inconsistency of copies due to error in replication process. b) System crash before manipulated data is written persistently to the file-system) by an application or operating system is written to storage.

  • Consistency of replica: Mechanisms must be in place that make sure, that copy and original are identical at the time of creation. Depending on the use case for a copy (synchronous/asynchronous mirror, backup see chapter Technology Basics) a copy might differ from the original as time goes by due to manipulation of the primary data.

Data consistency is a pre-requisite for proper operation of any application dealing with persistent data, i.e. data surviving the applications’ runtime. With respect to this requirement, data consistency is determined more precisely by the following two terms:

  • Application consistency: The application ensures, that all relevant data is written to persistent storage at certain points in time. Especially, this must be done before creating replica of data sets (e.g. backups), that shall be accessible by the application later. This requires application agents cooperating with any instance involved in copying data to storage.

  • File-System consistency: Operating systems can be regarded as special applications providing files to users (and to other applications). Therefore, the operating system ensures, that files and all related meta-data is written to persistent storage at certain points in time. This technology implies that agents in the operating system work together with instances involved in copying data to storage. In the context of virtualization this is often referred to as guest quiescing.

The described objectives address two risks for data:

  • Outages of the storage infrastructure: Any single point of failure of the storage infrastructure could lead to the unavailability of data. Failures include the storage network, storage systems or data center sites.

  • Data corruption: Corruption can be caused by technical failures as shown in Figure 4 : a) Inconsistency of copies due to error in replication process. b) System crash before manipulated data is written persistently to the file-system. In addition to that, data can be corrupted by the intentional or unintentional manipulation by a user.

  1. Levels of Data Availability

The availability requirements for data are differentiated as follows:

  • Dispensable data means that no specific requirements for data availability exist.

  • Regular availability means that on top of the mechanisms implemented for dispensable data, a secondary instance of data (e.g. backup) is available to compensate for outages of the primary storage.

  • High availability means that in addition to the measures implemented for regular availability, application consistent backups are created with small RPO. Therefore, the application must provide mechanisms that can interact with those backup systems. A restore from a backup with short RTO must be possible.

  • Constant availability means that in addition to the requirements for high availability the data is protected by mechanisms for continuous data protection.

  1. Technology Basics

The basic element of the CG IS Architecture is a hardware stack that represents a building block consisting of one or multiple physical servers, of storage and connecting network elements.

  1. Virtualization

In virtualization environments, multiple hosts are typically grouped in a so called “compute cluster”. It is possible to manage the infrastructure from a central management facility.

Load balancing within compute clusters is realized by several mechanisms: In any case, virtual machines (VMs) may be moved from one physical server to another. Here, the VMs are moved with their entire memory contents (i.e. RAM) while continuously available. It is possible to move the VMs within one cluster but also across data center or cluster borders respectively. Besides, there are mechanisms to shift the VM’s storage components (i.e. Data Store) with continuous availability.

Rule sets for the placement of VMs on physical servers can be defined in a virtualization environment. Those rules limit load balancing e.g. to guarantee optimized communication between VMs and to reduce the impact of single host failure.

Integrated snapshot capabilities allow the creation of images that comprise the memory of a VM as well as its virtual hard drive under continuous operation.

  1. VM Failover and Synchronous VMs

There are two mechanisms to reduce the impact of failures of server hardware in virtualization environments: VM failover and synchronous VMs. Both mechanisms do not diminish the effects of software or logical faults within the VMs themselves. The main difference between both mechanisms is RTO (Recovery Time Objective). The brand name of the mechanisms may differ with the choice of virtualization environment.

5.2.1. Failover

The failover mechanism restarts the VMs on remaining hosts after hardware or software failures. Therefore, the virtualization environment monitors the hosts or VMs in a cluster. The latter is provided by mechanisms of the tools installed on the operating system of the VM.

Restart priorities for VMs can be defined to reduce the down-times of the most important applications. Additionally, the available standby resources can be monitored to ensure the defined failover level (i.e. amount of redundant hardware).

The applications are not available during the restart period. Existing network connections will be lost in the case of a failure. Hence VM failover is not stateful.

The simplest option for VM failover is to provide the necessary redundant compute and storage capacity at one data center site. To improve the availability of applications even in case of disaster, it is possible to provide compute and storage capacities redundantly at two distant data center sister sites as shown in Figure 5 : Redundant compute and storage capacities at DC sister sites. Two scenarios are typically distinguished for both options: In the active/active scenario both systems parts are online and the workloads are evenly distributed. In the active/passive scenario one hardware part is on hot-standby and is used in the disaster case only. In both cases a maximum utilization rate of 50% must not be exceeded to ensure full availability after a disaster event.

5.2.2. Synchronous VMs

A synchronous VM provides stateful failover. “Synchronous” means that the entire state information of a VM is continuously synchronized with a redundant VM on another physical server. In fact, both VMs run the same code at the same time. Hence, continuous operation is guaranteed in the event of a technical failure.

Consistent synchronicity demands high communications and processing power. Typically, virtualization solutions allow only a limited number of virtual CPUs (vCPUs) to be protected by synchronization. The overall number of synchronous VMs per cluster is limited as well.

  1. Storage

Various types of storage concepts are supported by the CG IS Architecture Framework. The vendor of the storage system is typically determined by the system that is already used at a location. Local staff is familiar with the features and tools of its “traditional” storage vendor.

Furthermore, the application criticality determines the availability and redundancy of the storage. Additionally, the data volume needs to be considered when the storage concept is evaluated.

 

5.3.1. Storage Types

Four types of storage are distinguished:

In case of Direct Attached Storage (DAS) the physical storage components (hard drives, optical discs, etc.) are directly connected to the physical server accessing it. This means that the storage is only assigned to one physical server.

Storage virtualization can be applied to combine DAS of different physical servers and increase availability by mirroring of data. Hyper-Converged Infrastructure (HCI) typically relies on virtualized DAS (vDAS), i.e. the DAS of individual hosts is shared amongst the cluster. Storage is provisioned either directly by the hypervisor or by the (virtual) appliance controlling the vDAS cluster. This allows the implementation of mechanisms for high availability.

In Storage Area Networks (SAN) storage is managed with block-based protocols on centralized storage arrays and may be shared by several compute clusters. The physical devices, such as disk arrays or tape libraries, are separated from the physical servers by a dedicated network. Logical storage resources are provided for the attached hosts by logical unit numbers (LUN). Block-based protocols, i.e. Fiber Channel (FC), Fiber Channel over Ethernet (FCoE) and Internet Small Computer System Interface (iSCSI), are supported in common virtualization environments. SANs are used to enhance both performance and utilization of the storage resources.

In contrast to SAN, Network-Attached Storage (NAS) use centralized storage instances that provide and manage a file system for the stored data. Accordingly, the file system is hosted on the storage components. File-based transitions are used to provide data access for the hosts and may also be shared by several compute clusters. The file-based storage protocols Network File System (NFS) and Server Message Block/Common Internet File System (SMB/CIFS) are supported by most virtualization solutions.

State of the art virtualization solutions support all common types of direct attached storage, virtualized direct attached storage, storage area networks and network attached storage. Application Programming Interfaces (APIs) of modern storage technologies allow the management of storage at the virtual machine level. This simplifies storage provisioning and administration and allows the reservation of resources for individual VMs.

The decision between file- and block-based storage protocols depends on use case, performance (bandwidth, latency) and security considerations.

5.3.2. Data Storage

The storage concept deals with the availability and consistency of stored data. These requirements are met by redundant components within one or two data centers. Data is replicated either by mechanisms provided by the storage system itself (storage mirror) or by the software (OS-based or application mirror). Storage mirror mechanisms are provided by all the major storage vendors to replicate data on redundant storage arrays. Storage mirroring can also be realized in vDAS clusters.

E.g. an application mirror is provided by the database vendor and is hence independent of the underlying storage.

Mirroring of data can either by carried out synchronous or asynchronous. In case of synchronous mirroring, a storage transaction is confirmed by the storage appliance once the data is stored on both instances. This mechanism guarantees consistent mirroring of the data. In the case of a disaster, only data in the memory and CPU cache will be lost. Thus, synchronous mirroring is the most demanding storage mechanism when determining the maximum latency of the involved components. If lower demands for consistency can be assumed or local restrictions like large distances of datacenters prohibit synchronous mirroring, asynchronous mirroring could be an option. The storage operation is confirmed once the data is stored on the primary device. Mirroring to the second facility is carried out afterwards. Hence this mechanism reduces the demand for low latency transactions. In case of an asynchronous mirror, the RPO can never be zero since data in the synchronization-time-interval will be lost in case of a failure.

5.3.3. Virtualized Direct Attached Storage

The main idea of virtualized DAS is to improve storage availability by sharing DAS of distributed physical servers. The storage is shared amongst physical servers without the need for a dedicated storage platform. Using this approach, performance and availability of dedicated storage arrays can be obtained by using DAS.

Compute capacity is required for vDAS management. The necessary capacity depends on the storage features that are activated. Mechanisms like deduplication or compression consume more RAM and CPU resources than the provisioning of raw storage capacity. These resources must be provided in addition to those necessary for the applications. This explicitly includes implementation of capacity management as described in measure M-1-3-02. Compute monitoring.

  1. Backup and Restore

When speaking about consistency and availability of backups, Recovery Point Objective (RPO) and Recovery Time Objective (RTO) have to be taken into account. Besides the general definition of RPO and RTO in Chapter Definition of Terms their relationship to backup and restore must be considered (see Figure 6 : RPO and RTO resulting from an incident):

  • RPO is highly related to the frequency of data replication. Shorter RPO can be achieved by reducing the time periods between the generations of recovery points.

  • RTO is related to the speed by which data can be restored. RTO is long for retrieving backups from tape libraries and short for states retrieved from disk based storage. Besides, RTO depends on the process of restarting the application environment until it is fully available to users. In most cases, backup-stacking is carried out to create recovery points for an application. For example, backups of the bootable OS, the VM itself, a database and of the database redo logs might be necessary to restore an application. Therefore, RTO is highly dependent on the application and the extent of damage to the infrastructure.

Note that only backup media stored on-site ensure a short RTO. On the other hand, backup media should be stored off-site for disaster protection. Therefore, data should be classified according to its availability requirements (see below).

5.4.1. Mechanisms

The requirements for data availability described in chapter Definition of Protection Requirements for Applications are addressed by a variety of technologies. The appropriate measures need to be chosen according to the defined level of data availability (chapter Definition of Protection Requirements for Applications) for an application.

  • Mirroring continuously generates redundant data on different devices. Primarily, mirroring provides redundant sets of online data in case of a hardware failure within the storage system. Storage Mirroring does not protect from data corruption because any false data will immediately be copied to the mirrored device. Note that this mechanism does not protect from data loss due to accidental or malicious deletion of data or logical errors in the application.

  • Backup allows the restore of a consistent dataset after outage, corruption or manipulation of the primary storage. A backup consists of two steps: First a consistent dataset is generated (DC, FC or AC). Second this dataset is replicated to another medium (media disruption). Typically, the first step includes several operations (backup stacking) to obtain a backup that is used to restore a service. Amongst other things, a backup of the bootable OS, the VM and the database must be stored. Efficiency increases when the number of backup operations is reduced. Media disruption ensures that data can be recovered even after a storage system fails. The interpretation of the term media disruption depends on requirements. It can range from replicating to a secondary storage system of the same vendor to a completely different storage technology e.g. tapes.

  • Data Protection (DP) gives the possibility for fast restore of consistent data (file system consistency or application consistency) in case of data corruption or accidental data loss. Data Protection is usually carried out by periodic execution of snapshots on the primary storage medium. Keep in mind, that data protection only addresses the loss of data. It does protect against loss of media containing that data. This requirement is addressed by backups. The typical RPO (term defined in the CG IS Architecture Framework) for data protection is 5 minutes or longer.

  • Continuous Data Protection (CDP) reduces the RPO achieved by DP while it still allows fast restore of consistent data (file system consistency or application consistency). CDP creates redundant data with a minimum deviation to the productive data set. Typically, RPOs shorter than 1 minute are possible. CDP is implemented either by vendor specific technologies or the combination of periodic snapshots with script-based replication.

It is important to note that each flavor of consistency described in Chapter Definition of Protection Requirements for Applications can be achieved with backup, DP and CDP. To obtain File-System consistency or Application consistency, an integration with the operating system or the application is necessary. Mirroring, on the contrary provides a redundant dataset that cannot be used for a restore. Nevertheless, a storage mirror ensures identical datasets on both sides instantaneously (synchronous mirror) or after a certain period of time (asynchronous mirror).

  1. Backup of the data store: The storage API and a dedicated virtual backup proxy machine are used to generate images of the VMs. Crash-consistent backups of Windows-based VMs are possible by mechanisms of the Volume Shadow Copy Services (VSS). Backup of the application data “In Guest”. Application-consistent backups are pulled directly from the applications by application-specific backup agents.

  1. Network Zoning

Security is an important topic for Daimler data centers regarding network infrastructure and production. Various malware ‘attacks’ may already be spread across infected devices and might affect production networks. Therefore, separation of so-called “security areas” is mandatory. Such a separation applies to data centers because there are typical services offered to devices located in different security areas. Since services correspond to VMs, there must be some means to operate VMs of different security areas on CG IS stacks. The following paragraphs outline principles of zoning networks and VMs.

5.6.1. Virtual LANs

Networks at Daimler regional data centers are mostly based on so-called Fabrics. The goal here is to provide a layer-2-capable transport on top of the router-based network. Indeed, fabrics are based on routing protocols. But unlike IP routing, fabrics will route MAC addresses instead of IP-based addresses. From an endpoint’s (client or server) perspective, fabrics behave like ordinary layer-2 switching equipment spread over entire data centers (and their sister sites). In other word, fabrics support all kinds of virtual LANs (VLANs). Figure 8 : VLANs configured on a fabric visualizes that approach: Servers of different VLANs are connected to the same underlying fabric.

  1. Figure 8 : VLANs configured on a fabric

For smaller data centers, for instance at Daimler plants, the fabric approach is somehow oversized. Instead, typical layer-2 switching equipment is used and VLANs are configured on these switches manually.

Physical servers connect to VLANs using VLAN tagging. Multiple VLANs are transported within a single Ethernet if Frames are tagged respectively. The hypervisor residing on a physical server will assort those tagged frames into the appropriate VLAN of its virtual switch. VMs are then configured to connect to one or more VLANs of the virtual switch.

  1. Figure 9 : VLANs inside hypervisors

Network zones can be seen as an equivalent to VLANs from a VM perspective.

5.6.2. Zones

Zones are groups of VLANs that share the same security level. In other words, communication between zones can only happen via firewalls or similar network security devices. A defined ruleset on each of the security devices is required in order to establish communication.

Zones in Daimler regional data centers are grouped according to their purpose and called “Pods”. Examples are Pods for SAP or for internet-facing applications. Pods usually are implemented on a fabric. Zones in smaller data centers are made of VLANs connected to Virtual Routing and Forwarding (VRF) instances.

However, even within the same zone, the servers are not necessarily allowed to communicate to each other. Restricting communication between servers of the same zone generally is useful whenever they are multi-homed. Consider for instance a backup zone connecting all servers with a central backup facility (as depicted in Figure 10 : Servers of different zones connected to a common backup zone). In this case, the backup zone could behave as a short circuit between servers of different zones. In other words, communication between these servers could happen via the backup zone instead the regular path, via firewalls. Therefore, restricting communication inside zones is mandatory. This restriction is achieved by functionality of Private VLANs.

  1. Figure 10 : Servers of different zones connected to a common backup zone

5.6.3. Private VLANs

With Layer-2 switching every device connected to a VLAN may communicate with each other in the same VLAN. If unrestricted communication is not intended (see above) Private VLAN functionality may be configured.

Generally, Private VLAN is a subset of ordinary VLANs. Ports designated to private VLANs may be configured as listed below (see Figure 11 : Communication within Private VLANs):

  • Promiscuous Ports do not restrict communication at all. They may communicate to any other port being part of the primary VLAN. This includes any ports in the subordinate PVLANs. Promiscuous ports are typically used to connect gateways or routers.

  • Isolated Ports are not able to communicate anywhere except to Promiscuous Ports.

  • Community Ports connect to Community VLANs. Every port of any Community VLAN may communicate to any other port of the same Community VLAN and to Promiscuous Ports.

  1. Figure 11 : Communication within Private VLANs

5.6.4. Security Zones inside CG IS Stacks

Security zones at Daimler data centers are implemented by means of VLAN techniques. The following requirements must be met by CG IS Architecture Framework stacks:

  • Virtual switches provided by the hypervisor must support Private VLANs, i.e. Community and Isolated PVLANs.

  • There must be an option to extend Private VLANs to physical switching equipment. This should be implemented by VLAN tagging and appropriate configuration of such links.

  • Physical switching equipment inside blade enclosures and as part of Converged and Hyper-Converged Infrastructure must support Private VLANs as well.

  • The physical and virtual switch design inside CG IS stacks must ensure that security requirements are met.

In fact, secure administration of stack switches is crucial as responsibility might not be assigned clearly. Managing security zones is usually within the responsibility of the network infrastructure staff. However, since virtual switches are part of the hypervisor layer the responsibility could shift to server operations.

Secure administration of zones inside CG IS stacks is the result of well-defined responsibilities between network and server staff.

  1. Measures

Once risks have been identified and assessed, measures to avoid or to mitigate the risks must be determined and implemented.

Measures that mitigate a risk completely either reduce the probability of occurrence to almost zero or they reduce the impact in a way, that business continuity is not affected by the occurrence of the risk. Such measures are classified as “D” or “I” in the cross reference table. Measures that are classified with a “D” directly address the respective risk. Measures classified with an “I” are not primarily selected to mitigate the risk. However, the measures marked with an “I” still fully cover probability of occurrence or impact of the risk.

Measures marked with an “R” reduce either probability of occurrence or impact of a risk. Measures marked with an “M” are mandatory for CG IS stacks.

  1. M-1-1-01. Use of components with high tolerance for environmental conditions

Fully Covered:

Impact/probability of occurrence reduced: R-1-03. Malfunction of DC building infrastructure , R-2-01. Malfunction of network connectivity (LAN/SAN), R-2-04. Failure / outage of stack hosts, R-2-05. Failure / outage of single storage controller, R-2-06. Failure / outage of single storage unit

Typically, stacks are positioned within data center sites providing adequate, fully available air conditioning that guarantees normal range of temperature, humidity, dust, vibration etc. at any time.

Fully Covered:

Outages due to improper stack components should be prevented. Exclusive use of Daimler shopping cart items will ensure that any component is approved.

The shopping card items provide a solid basis to run guest OS, middleware and applications. The risk that a problem occurs for the first time at a specific implementation is reduced since typically shopping cart items are operated at a large number of Daimler sites. Besides, troubleshooting may be accomplished in a shorter time due to central support.

Fully Covered: Malfunction of single DC power circuit (D), R-2-05. Failure / outage of single storage controller (D)

Impact/probability of occurrence reduced:

Outages due to technical failures of individual components should be prevented. In order to minimize those impacts critical stack hardware components are provisioned redundantly. Thus, hosts and storage units are equipped with redundant

  • Power supplies connected to different power circuits,

  • Fans, and

  • Internal network connections in case of converged infrastructure providing network elements.

  • Storage controllers

  1. M-1-1-04. Mixed-vendor strategy

Fully Covered: R-2-12. Calamity of stack infrastructure (non-fixable software/firmware failure, demise of products/manufacturer) (D), R-3-01. Malfunction of software / guest OS (D)

Impact/probability of occurrence reduced: R-2-08. Software failure stack management (stack inoperative)

In case of non-fixable bugs in essential software or firmware functions or short-dated end of life of infrastructure components, the mixed- vendor strategy allows efficient changeover of infrastructure.

Furthermore, mixed-vendor strategy could be advantageous if both vendors are used in the same environment. If for instance a pair of clustered VMs is running on different hypervisors, this reduces the risk of outages due to software failures within a hypervisor instance of one manufacturer.

  1. M-1-1-05. Redundant components spread over independent sections of same DC site

Fully Covered: Environmental disaster affecting parts of DC, (D), Malfunction of single DC power circuit (I), R-1-05. Malfunction of DC cabling (I)

Impact/probability of occurrence reduced:

Outages of entire stack due to hardware failures should be prevented as far as possible. Therefore, stacks hosting important applications should be built by using redundant hosts. Those hosts should be evenly distributed amongst different areas within the DC. The areas should be located in different disaster protection areas in order to prevent damage caused by local disaster like local fire.

In case of outage of the primary instance of application, the applications can be restarted from the redundant application instance.

8.1.2. Network

  1. M-1-2-01. Network connection with high performance

Fully Covered: R-2-02. Deterioration of LAN communication by storage communication or vice versa (D)

Impact/probability of occurrence reduced: R-4-08. Network congestion (LAN) , R-4-09. Network congestion (SAN)

In order to avoid network congestion in infrastructure, the network connections should be implemented with high bandwidth, meaning that a considerable amount of free bandwidth is available in normal operation. The measure is only effective when combined with network monitoring.

Multiple links can be aggregated to one logical high-performance link (Link Aggregation Group, LAG). All links within the LAG are in active status and are used with load balancing.

  1. M-1-2-02. Redundant network connections within stack (compute and storage)

Fully Covered: Malfunction / outage of network component (LAN/SAN) (D)

Impact/probability of occurrence reduced: R-4-02. Long lasting restore

Network connections within stack should be redundantly implemented in order to prevent Single Point of Failures within the stack. The redundancy mechanisms comprise

  • Redundant network interfaces of hosts and storage units

  • Redundant network elements for LAN and SAN within stack in case of Converged Infrastructure

  • Full-meshed connections between those elements.

In any case, automated failover or active/active usage of the redundant connections is implemented to allow a RTO of almost zero.

  1. M-1-2-03. Connection of stack to redundant network elements

Fully Covered: R-1-05. Malfunction of DC cabling (D), R-2-01. Malfunction of network connectivity (LAN/SAN) Malfunction / outage of network interface (LAN/SAN) (D), R-2-01. Malfunction of network connectivity (LAN/SAN) Malfunction / outage of network component (LAN/SAN) (I)

Impact/probability of occurrence reduced: R-4-02. Long lasting restore

Network connection is one essential element of a stack. Within the data center, communication of host and storage systems often rely on the general network that is part of the DC facilities, i.e.

  • Data access from applications use those network connections to storage via LAN or SAN

  • Compute or storage cluster use those network connections to synchronize the cluster elements.

Therefore, in addition to redundant network connections within the stack, the hosts should be connected to redundant network elements via redundant network interfaces.

  1. M-1-2-04. Redundant stack connection between DC sites

Fully Covered: Malfunction of single interconnection between DC sites (D)

Impact/probability of occurrence reduced:

The stack should be redundantly connected between DC sister sites in order to provided high availability of the entire stack. Therefore, both the LAN and Fiber Channel SAN connections should be redundantly provided; Fiber Channel must be provided in a dual-fabric design.

  1. M-1-2-05. Full-redundant stack connection between DC sites

Fully Covered: Malfunction of single interconnection between DC sites (I), Outage of entire network connection between DC sites (D)

Impact/probability of occurrence reduced:

In order to avoid split brain of the stack, the stack connection between the DC sister sites should be implemented with full redundancy without any Single Point of Failure. This should be provided even if one DC site is positioned outside the Daimler premises or if the connection is built-up via public property. This full-redundant connection may rely on

  • Different providers/carriers providing different Points of Present for redundant connections,

  • Different infeed points to DC sites and

  • Fully disjunct cable paths on Daimler and public premises.

  1. M-1-2-06. Network monitoring

Fully Covered:

Mandatory Measure

Addressed Risks: R-2-02. Deterioration of LAN communication by storage communication or vice versa, R-4-08. Network congestion (LAN), R-4-09. Network congestion (SAN)

Initially, network links to connect stacks are provided with enough capacity in order to guarantee continuous availability. In the course of time applications change their communication profile or new applications are hosted on a stack. Continuous network management of stack connection ensures that the capacity reserves remains within the requested range and that redundant links are still working. If the capacity is no longer sufficient, the stacks must be upgraded, i.e. additional links must be added or link capacity must be increased. Without monitoring of network capacity other measures like M-1-2-01. Network connection with high performance are not working.

Network redundancy helps to reduce outages of the network connection if single components fail. It is important to continuously monitor the health (e.g. general availability, infrastructure health) to enable fast replacement or repair of failed components. Without continuous monitoring the redundancy mechanisms do not provide their full potential. Outages after the initial failure of a single component cannot be compensated if the failed unit is not replaced fast enough. Monitored characteristics include:

  • Network capacity utilization

  • Network availability

Continuous monitoring allows the detection of anomalies and thus potential IT attacks or failures. Hence, problem identification and troubleshooting are accelerated.

  1. M-1-2-07. Logical separation of network interfaces within compute network (LAN)

Fully Covered:

Impact/probability of occurrence reduced: R-4-08. Network congestion (LAN)

The network interfaces of a host are used for different communication types that are subject to different requirements concerning security and network load. For example, moving virtual machines between physical servers of a virtualization cluster will cause network traffic peaks. It must be guaranteed, that such peaks do neither influence nor degrade traffic flow from applications to users or vice versa. Additionally, such traffic must not interfere with management access to any element either.

Thus the network interfaces should be logically separated in order to implement resource control and to enforce security aspects. At least, the following communication types should be logically separated:

  • Management network for infrastructure management (e.g. hypervisor, iLO),

  • vMotion and

  • User network

The separation reduces the risk of compromising attacks, especially via management communication, and improves the stability of both networks.

Note: The management covers out-band management of infrastructure resources, e.g. hardware components or hypervisor, but does not cover management of any layer above the hypervisor, e.g. the operating system.

Impact/probability of occurrence reduced: R-4-02. Long lasting restore

In order to enhance performance and to minimize impairment, specific communication should be physically separated within the host. Therefore, dedicated physical interfaces should be implemented for communication of

  • Management

  • vMotion and

  • User network

The separation considerably reduces the risk of compromising attacks, especially via management communication, and improves the stability of communication.

8.1.3. Compute

  1. M-1-3-01. Allocation of dedicated hardware resources (compute and storage) for applications within one stack

Fully Covered: R-3-02. Performance impact due to overbooking of resources

Impact/probability of occurrence reduced: R-4-10. Compute congestion , R-4-11. Storage congestion

Virtualization introduces the most benefit whenever resources are broadly shared by consuming applications. However, high reliability and availability may demand dedicated mapping of resources to applications.

Therefore, dedicated hardware resources like compute and storage capacity should be allocated for applications with high requirements regarding reliability and availability. This is also true for blade server technology. Dedicated blade chassis for application with high requirements and the avoidance of physical servers with native OS reduce the risk for overbooking of the shared mid- or backplane of a blade chassis and that of the external network connections.

Note: The use of “resource pools” provided by virtualization instances does not provide the same level of risk mitigation.

  1. M-1-3-02. Compute monitoring

Mandatory Measure

Addressed Risks: R-3-02. Performance impact due to overbooking of resources , R-4-07. Accidental placement of software on stack , R-4-10. Compute congestion

Initially, computing capacity of the physical servers is configured with enough overcapacity in order to guarantee continuous availability. In the course of time, continuous capacity management ensures that the capacity reserves of the stack remain within the requested range. If the capacity is no longer sufficient the existing stack must be upgraded or a new stack is to be added. Capacity management includes:

  • Utilization of compute capacity (CPU cores and memory)

  • Utilization of server network I/O capacity (LAN)

  • Availability of compute hosts

The maximum degree of utilization needs to be considered prior to the deployment of a new application. Sufficient redundancy of compute capacity (M-1-3-03. n+1 redundancy of compute capacity and M-1-3-04. 2n redundancy of compute capacity) has to be guaranteed as well as the functionality of disaster recovery plans.

Continuous monitoring allows the detection of anomalies and thus potential IT attacks or failures. Hence, problem identification and troubleshooting are accelerated.

  1. M-1-3-03. n+1 redundancy of compute capacity

Fully Covered: Failure / outage of single host of stack (D)

Impact/probability of occurrence reduced: R-4-02. Long lasting restore

Scenarios like planned maintenance, technical failures of individual components or disaster events need to be considered when engineering a hardware stack. To minimize the impact of a capacity loss, sufficient compute resources have to be provisioned. Therefore, computing capacity as well as I/O performance of the hosts resp. the Converged Infrastructure are configured with overcapacity in order to guarantee continuous availability even in case of failures.

According to Figure 17 : Continuous operation after failure of a single element in a system with n+1 redundancy, n+1 redundancy leads to continuous operation after the failure of a single element. Consider for instance compute nodes of a hardware stack running several virtual machines. If one node fails, the remaining nodes will take over all virtual machines of the defective node. n+1 redundancy implies, that sufficient resources are available to serve all virtual machines without an (performance) impact.

Besides, n+1 redundancy allow for hardware maintenance without affecting any application running on the hardware stack.

Keep in mind, that n+1 redundancy will not ensure continuous operation if two or more elements fail.

If capacity management (see M-1-3-02. Compute monitoring) indicates that n+1 redundancy is no longer possible due to additional applications the existing stack must be upgraded by new hosts or a new stack must be added.

  1. M-1-3-04. 2n redundancy of compute capacity

Fully Covered: Failure / outage of multiple hosts of stack (D), Failure / outage of single host of stack

Impact/probability of occurrence reduced: R-4-02. Long lasting restore

In case of critical applications, e.g. CC applications, n+1 redundancy of the elements might not be sufficient. For such applications, RTO near zero needs to be guaranteed even in the case of multiple failures. Even the outage of half of the hosts does not impede sufficient infrastructure for full operation due to the 2n redundancy of all elements.

  1. Figure 18 : Continuous operation after multiple failure in an environment with 2n redundancy

If capacity management indicates that 2n redundancy is no longer possible due to additional applications the existing stack must be upgraded or a new stack must be added. Keep in mind that in practical installations maximum resource utilization shall not exceed 40% to maintain application performance in case of failure.

  1. M-1-3-05. Secondary compute cluster

Fully Covered: R-2-10. Storage overload by continuous data protection (D), R-3-09. Failure of hypervisor (I)

Impact/probability of occurrence reduced:

To allow two application instances to run in parallel independent of the underlying hardware a secondary compute cluster can be installed. In case e.g. an update of the management server of one virtualization cluster leads to a full outage of this cluster the application is still functioning on the secondary cluster.

  1. M-1-3-06. VM failover with active/passive scenario

Fully Covered: Service outage due to manual restart of VM (RTO >> 0) (D), Service outage due to manual restart of application (RTO >>0)(D)

Impact/probability of occurrence reduced: R-4-02. Long lasting restore , R-4-04. Manipulation / intentional destruction of hardware

To allow quick recovery of an application that is hosted on a stack, failover mechanisms should be implemented within the cluster. They allow restarting VMs on surviving members of a cluster. Hence, RTO is minimized. However, the failover requires a restart of the VM which implicates that the application is not available during stateless restart and that the network connections must be restarted.

  1. M-1-3-07. VM failover with active/active scenario

Fully Covered: Service outage due to restart of VM (RTO > 0) (D), Service outage due to manual restart of VM (RTO >> 0) (I), Service outage due to restart of application (RTO >0) (D), Service outage due to manual restart of application (RTO >>0) (I)

Impact/probability of occurrence reduced: R-4-02. Long lasting restore , R-4-04. Manipulation / intentional destruction of hardware

Continuous availability of applications is achieved by operating fully synchronized VMs within a cluster of physically separated hosts. If the host with the primary VM fails, the secondary VM can take over. As both VMs run the same code at the same time, the failover is stateful, ie. no connection is lost in the event of a failover.

To allow synchronized VMs, twice the resources of a single VM are continuously consumed. In addition, transactions need to be synchronized over the network.

Disadvantage: high communications and processing power necessary, only limited number of synchronous VMs per cluster

  1. M-1-3-08. Identical compute nodes

Fully Covered:

Impact/probability of occurrence reduced: R-2-11. Software failure of hypervisor cluster, R-4-12. Slow problem management

Virtual Machines in a cluster are moving between hosts in case of a failure, due to load balancing or for maintenance reasons. This migration is typically not possible between hosts using different CPU generations since the CPUs feature sets are typically not identical. Mechanisms of the virtualization environment that overcome this limitation are typically connected to performance degradations. Hence, optimal performance and VM mobility is guaranteed by compute nodes with identical CPU generations inside a cluster.

  1. M-1-3-09. Dedicated compute capacity for virtualized DAS

Fully Covered:

Impact/probability of occurrence reduced: R-2-09. Storage overload (performance degradation of application), R-4-10. Compute congestion , R-4-11. Storage congestion

The virtualization of DAS allows the distribution of data amongst servers and hence highly available storage. In addition, mechanisms for data reduction like deduplication and compression can be applied. In a vDAS installation the required resources are provided by the servers. Dedicated resources for storage control are to be provided in order to avoid negative effect (compute congestion) on application performance caused by vDAS control or vice versa.

8.1.4. Storage

  1. M-1-4-01. Asynchronous storage mirroring

Fully Covered: R-2-05. Failure / outage of single storage controller (I), Failure / outage of single storage unit, all data lost (D)

Impact/probability of occurrence reduced: R-4-01. Accidental deletion of data

Data replication resp. storage mirroring guarantees 2n redundancy, even in case of single site data center. As shown in Figure 20 : Storage mirroring on a single data center site, continuous operation is then guaranteed in the event of a failure of a storage component. As illustrated in Figure 21 : Distribution of resources amongst two data center sister sites the storage resources can also be distributed amongst two data centers.

With asynchronous data replication, storage operation is confirmed after the data is stored on the primary device; then mirroring to the second storage instance is carried out afterwards.

Data will be lost during the synchronization time interval with asynchronous data replication, i.e. RPO will be greater than 0 in any case. This feature can also be advantageous since e.g. in case of an accidental deletion of data: Aborting the synchronization process before data replication is carried out allows the restore from the replication target.

Note: Asynchronous OS-based or application-based mirroring may substitute or enhance storage mirroring depending on protection requirements of the respective application.

  1. Figure 21 : Distribution of resources amongst two data center sister sites

  1. M-1-4-02. Synchronous storage mirroring

Fully Covered: Failure outage of single storage unit, data loss (D), Failure / outage of single storage unit, all data lost (I), R-2-05. Failure / outage of single storage controller (I)

Impact/probability of occurrence reduced:

With synchronous mirroring, i.e. data replication, the storage transaction is done to both instances simultaneously; the storage appliance confirms the storage transaction after the data is stored on both instances. Thus, synchronous mirroring guarantees RPO near 0.

Synchronous data replication requires 2n redundancy of storage capacity.

At least, synchronous mirroring is carried out single-site, i.e. the storage is hosted in two distinct parts of one data center.

Note: Synchronous OS-based or application-based mirroring may substitute or enhance storage mirroring depending on protection requirements of the respective application.

  1. M-1-4-03. Physical separation of resources

Fully Covered: R-2-09. Storage overload (performance degradation of application) (D)

Impact/probability of occurrence reduced: R-4-04. Manipulation / intentional destruction of hardware, R-4-11. Storage congestion

Physical separation of resources in a storage system ensures that dedicated performance and availability is guaranteed for applications. Typically, physical separation is applied for resources like hard-drives while storage controllers are shared.

  1. M-1-4-04. Support for continuous data protection

Fully Covered: R-2-10. Storage overload by continuous data protection (D)

Impact/probability of occurrence reduced: R-2-09. Storage overload (performance degradation of application)

Support for storage snapshots or the replication of storage I/O and the cooperation of the application allow CDP.

  1. M-1-4-05. Storage monitoring

Fully Covered:

Mandatory Measure: R-2-09. Storage overload (performance degradation of application), R-4-09. Network congestion (SAN), R-4-11. Storage congestion

Initially, storage capacity as well as I/O performance of the storage units and available SAN-Bandwidth is configured with enough overcapacity in order to guarantee continuous availability. In the course of time, continuous capacity management ensures that the capacity reserves of the storage system remains within the requested range. If the capacity is no longer sufficient the existing storage must be enhanced. The monitored characteristics include:

  • Storage capacity utilization

  • Utilization of I/O capacity of the storage system

  • SAN capacity utilization

  • SAN and storage availability

The maximum degree of utilization needs to be considered prior to the deployment of a new application.

Storage redundancy helps to reduce outages of storage or SAN-systems if a single component fails. It is important to continuously monitor the health (e.g. general availability, infrastructure health) to enable fast replacement or repair of failed components. Without continuous monitoring the redundancy mechanisms do not provide their full potential. Outages after the initial failure of a single component cannot be compensated if the failed unit is not replaced fast enough.

Continuous monitoring allows the detection of anomalies and thus potential IT attacks or failures. Hence, problem identification and troubleshooting are accelerated.

8.1.5. Backup and Restore

  1. M-1-5-01. Backup

Fully Covered: Failure / outage of entire storage cluster, all data lost (D), Data loss due to corruption (D),R-2-11. Software failure of hypervisor cluster, R-4-01. Accidental deletion of data (D), R-4-04. Manipulation / intentional destruction of hardware (R), R-4-05. Manipulation / intentional deletion of data (D), R-4-06. Malware infection (D), R-3-09. Failure of hypervisor (D)

Impact/probability of occurrence reduced:

Backups to separate media ensure that data is available after any hardware or software failure of the storage systems. The achievable RTO depends on the performance of the backup system as well as its connection to the network.

Backup is carried out by replication to an additional backup medium. This could be accomplished by data transfer to separate medium, e.g. tapes, or by asynchronous mirroring of data to a remote storage device (e.g. at one of Daimler’s regional data centers).

The interdependence of application instances that use different data stores and datasets implies that consistency of data is not automatically guaranteed by backups. To allow a restore after data corruption, backups of consistent datasets are needed. Therefore, data consistency must be provided by cooperation of storage system and application.

If clusters are based on single-site storage, backup media should be stored at a remote backup site to allow disaster recovery.

To allow a full restore in any failure all relevant parts that are necessary to resume operation must be part of the backup. Amongst others, this might include the hypervisor, central management of the virtualization cluster, bootable configuration of a virtual machine and application data.

  1. M-1-5-02. High-speed Backup

Fully Covered: Service Outage due to slow restore of lost data (RTO very large)

Impact/probability of occurrence reduced: R-4-02. Long lasting restore

The restore from a backup typically requires a significant amount of time that depends on factors like amount of data to be restored, available bandwidth of the backup medium and available network bandwidth. To ensure, that the required RTO can be met after a failure it is important to connect the backup systems with sufficient bandwidth. To determine the sufficient bandwidth continuous monitoring of the amount of data that needs to be restored is necessary. In addition to that, the backup system itself must be able to provide the data in the required time. The available bandwidth of a backup system can be increased e.g. by using faster media (HDDs instead of tape) or by increasing the number of media that can be accessed in parallel. 

  1. M-1-5-03. File System consistent recovery points

Fully Covered: Restore of files impossible due to corruption of filesystem

Impact/probability of occurrence reduced:

In case of data corruption or after a disaster, a recovery from replicated data is carried out. To allow the restore of a consistent file system the replica used for restore must be file system consistent. This can be achieved by quiescing the guest OS during the backup

  1. M-1-5-04. Application consistent recovery points

Fully Covered: Restore of application impossible due to corrupted data (D), Restore of files impossible due to corruption of filesystem (I)

Impact/probability of occurrence reduced:

In case of data corruption or after a disaster, a recovery from replicated data is carried out. To allow application consistency for the restored data, the replica must be created in cooperation with the application. This can be achieved by agents in the application that communicate with the backup system.

  1. M-1-5-05. Data protection

Fully Covered: Data loss due to corruption (I), Data loss due to corruption since last backup (RPO > 5 min) (D), R-4-01. Accidental deletion of data (I), R-4-06. Malware infection (I)

Impact/probability of occurrence reduced: R-4-05. Manipulation / intentional deletion of data

In case of accidental deletion of data or software failures, previous states of data must be restored. Restoring such data from backups takes time and will not fit RTO requirements. Therefore there must be a means to rollback to previous states within a short period of time. Typically, data protection is provided by the periodic generation of snapshots. RPOs in the order of 5 minutes can be achieved by data protection.

  1. M-1-5-06. Continuous data protection

Fully Covered: Data loss due to corruption (I), Data loss due to corruption since last backup (RPO > 5 min) (I), Data loss due to corruption since last recovery point (RPO > 1 min) (D), R-4-01. Accidental deletion of data (I), R-4-06. Malware infection (I)

Impact/probability of occurrence reduced: R-4-05. Manipulation / intentional deletion of data

Continuous data protection reduces the RPO of data protection further to below 1 minute. As shown in Figure 22 : Combination of periodic snapshots with copying of archive logs for continuous data protection., one implementation of continuous data protection is the combination of periodic snapshots with copies of data base archive logs. Another possible implementation is the duplication of write I/Os in combination with journaling that allows the rollback of the I/Os.

  1. Figure 22 : Combination of periodic snapshots with copying of archive logs for continuous data protection.

  1. M-1-5-07. Data protection to secondary storage

Fully Covered: Service Outage due to slow restore of lost data (RTO very large), Service Outage due to slow restore of lost data (RTO large)(I), Data loss due to corruption (I), Data loss due to corruption since last backup (RPO > 5 min) (I), R-4-01. Accidental deletion of data (I), R-4-02. Long lasting restore (I), R-4-06. Malware infection (I),

Impact/probability of occurrence reduced: R-4-04. Manipulation / intentional destruction of hardware, R-4-05. Manipulation / intentional deletion of data

In case of failure of entire primary storage there should be a means to start important applications anyway. This could be done using secondary storage. If Data Protection is destined to secondary storage data needed to restart applications is already available and needs not to be retrieved from backups.

  1. M-1-5-08. Application restart from backup medium

Fully Covered: Service Outage due to slow restore of lost data (RTO very large); Service Outage due to slow restore of lost data (RTO large)

Impact/probability of occurrence reduced:

In case of failure of entire primary storage there should be a means to start important applications anyway. This could be done using backup media with a performance that is comparable with that of the primary storage.

The performance and availability demands of important applications, e.g. CC applications, are typically well known. The possibility of unforeseen behavior during development of applications and integration of changes into the production environment is comparably high. Depending on the desired functionality of application instances, their average consumption of network resources differs. By separating application instances that are either consuming large network bandwidth or sending large amounts of packets per time interval, the utilization of network capacity can be optimized.

  1. M-1-6-04. Rules for manual split-brain handling

Fully Covered: Data inconsistency due to split brain (D)

Impact/probability of occurrence reduced:

The loss of connection between storage systems or the isolation of a host can lead to a split-brain situation. Without rules that regulate this situation, data could be manipulated by two (or more) instances that believe to be the last remaining.

Therefore, rules for split brain handling must be in place to determine the primary instance. If two storage systems loose connection those rules describe, which of the systems will remain operative in such a case. The decision for the instance that resumes production is taken manually. To avoid inconsistencies rules for automated stop of production (e.g. power off for hosts) in case of isolation must be implemented.

  1. M-1-6-05. Tie-breaker for automated split-brain handling

Fully Covered: Data inconsistency due to split brain (I), Service outage due to manual split-brain handling (D)

Impact/probability of occurrence reduced:

To avoid service outages due to manual intervention after isolation of systems automation of split-brain handling is implemented. A so called “tie breaker” (Figure 23 Tie breaker at a third location for automated split-brain handling) is used to determine whether a loss of connection is due to an outage of the secondary component or due to a loss of connection between the systems. Typically, the tie breaker is placed at an independent site and connected with small bandwidth. Latency of the connection to the tie breaker is not as important as between the productive systems.

  1. M-1-6-06. Rules to sustain performance of CC applications

Fully Covered:

Impact/probability of occurrence reduced: R-2-09. Storage overload (performance degradation of application) , R-3-02. Performance impact due to overbooking of resources , R-4-07. Accidental placement of software on stack , R-4-10. Compute congestion , R-4-11. Storage congestion

If BE and CC applications are placed on the same hardware stack, the availability of a CC application must be improved at least by installing rules to sustain its performance, e.g. in the case of compute congestion. Those rules include the controlled shutdown of uncritical applications in the case of an emergency.

Secondary storage typically provides less I/O performance and capacity than the primary storage. Hence, operation from secondary storage typically goes along with reduced performance. Accordingly, a rule set must be available to guarantee, that only the critical applications use the secondary storage in the disaster case.

  1. M-1-6-07. Rules for automatic placement of VMs

Fully Covered:

Impact/probability of occurrence reduced:  R-3-02. Performance impact due to overbooking of resources , R-4-07. Accidental placement of software on stack , R-4-10. Compute congestion , R-4-11. Storage congestion

The placement of VMs on hosts may influence the performance of applications. Since some applications rely on short round trip times between different VMs, placing the respective VMs into the same data center or even onto the same host will increase application performance. Such server instances could typically be application servers and databases.

However, placing VMs onto the same host could lead to service outages if the host fails for some reason.

Therefore, rules for automatic placement of VMs ensure stability and performance of applications.

  1. M-1-6-08. Optimized cluster size

Fully Covered:

Impact/probability of occurrence reduced: R-4-02. Long lasting restore, R-3-10. Failure with unacceptable impact

The impact of an outage is proportional to the number of critical systems on a failing cluster. In addition to that, the time it takes to restore failed systems increases with increasing number of affected virtual machines. Also, the complexity of a restore is raised if a lot of systems need to be recovered simultaneously.

Therefore, optimizing the cluster size helps to reduce the impact of outages. Typically, it is not recommended to place more than about 150 virtual machines on a single cluster.

If a large amount of data is lost e.g. after a failure of the primary storage typically a large number of individual steps are necessary to restore operation. Amongst others, restore of the infrastructure (e.g. hypervisors) and the virtual servers might be necessary. To guarantee that required RTOs are met it is important, that a fully tested restore and disaster recovery plan must be available. This plan must be known to all relevant operators. Regular tests of the restore and disaster recovery plan are necessary to make sure it will work in the case of an emergency.

  1. M-1-6-11. Automated Disaster Recovery

Typically, time is short if a restore is required after a major disaster. Restoring a complex environment with lots of interdependent services is challenging in such a situation even with a proven disaster recovery plan. Hence, automating the restore by appropriate tools will reduce the probability for errors and speed up the restore process.

  1. M-1-6-12. Advanced change management

Fully Covered:

Impact/probability of occurrence reduced: R-3-10. Failure with unacceptable impact, R-4-03. Accidental misconfiguration of stack components

Amongst others, a standardized, appropriate change process includes the task of informing responsible persons, e.g. the application owner, about a planned change. However, the exact date and time of a change might be defined in a certain window. If an incident is triggered by the change, the application owner might not be aware of the exact reason for some time. In such a situation troubleshooting and root cause identification are prolonged by a lack of knowledge.  Additional aspects like informing the application owner about every step of a change will decrease the impact of failures. Such measures are relevant especially for changes affecting CC applications.

  1. M-1-6-13. Dedicated resources for management and monitoring

Fully Covered:

Impact/probability of occurrence reduced: R-3-10. Failure with unacceptable impact, R-4-03. Accidental misconfiguration of stack components, R-4-13. Slow Problem identification

Failures of systems can only be detected efficiently if the monitoring itself is not affected by the failure. Combined outages of a cluster and associated management and monitoring systems increase the time it takes to identify the root cause of an outage and to restart systems. In addition to that, assessing counter measures for an outage is only possible if monitoring is still available. To reduce the duration of outages, management and monitoring infrastructure is placed on hardware that is independent of the systems that are managed or monitored respectively.

8.2.1. Mirroring

  1. M-2-1-01. OS-based asynchronous mirror

Fully Covered: R-2-05. Failure / outage of single storage controller (I), Failure / outage of single storage unit, all data lost (I)

Impact/probability of occurrence reduced:

Data mirroring carried out by software is applied to increase the availability of data and to avoid a single point of failure in the storage.

As shown in Figure 24 : OS-based mirroring OS-based mechanisms are implemented to replicate data to a secondary instance. Typically, this is realized by a distributed file system that is using the storage devices to store replica of the data. Storage of the secondary instance is completely independent of the primary storage to eliminate any single point of failure in the storage.

In addition, hardware-based storage mirrors may be applied to further increase availability of data.

  1. Figure 24 : OS-based mirroring

  1. M-2-1-02. OS-based synchronous mirror

Fully Covered: R-2-05. Failure / outage of single storage controller (I), Failure / outage of single storage unit, all data lost (I), Failure outage of single storage unit, data loss (I)

Impact/probability of occurrence reduced:

With synchronous mirror, the storage transaction is done to both instances simultaneously; the storage appliance confirms the storage transaction to the respective OS instance and the OS instances confirms successful transaction to application after the data is stored on both instances. Thus, synchronous mirroring guarantees RPO near 0.

  1. M-2-1-03. Application-based asynchronous mirror

Fully Covered: R-2-05. Failure / outage of single storage controller (I), Failure / outage of single storage unit, all data lost (I)

Impact/probability of occurrence reduced:

Application-based mirroring is applied to increase the availability of data and application as well as to avoid data corruption caused by the storage mirroring mechanism. Only data relevant for the applications is transmitted via the network. Hence, the overhead of application-based mirror is comparatively low. In addition, the consistency of data is checked at the application level where the data is manipulated. This reduces the risk for data corruption compared to storage or OS based mirrors.

As shown in Figure 25 : Application-based mirroring, application-based mechanisms are implemented to replicate data to a secondary application instance. The replicated data is then stored on a secondary storage device. Hence, mechanisms of the application are available to check the consistency of replicated data. Storage of the secondary instance is completely independent of the primary storage to eliminate any single point of failure in the storage.

A secondary instance of the application also guarantees availability of a failover instance in the case of hardware failure. The involved mechanisms use a fully functional “standby instance” which can take over the application load in the case of a failure.

In addition, hardware-based storage mirrors may be applied to further increase availability of data.

  1. Figure 25 : Application-based mirroring

  1. M-2-1-04. Application-based synchronous mirror

Fully Covered: R-2-05. Failure / outage of single storage controller (I), Failure / outage of single storage unit, all data lost (I), Failure outage of single storage unit, data loss (I)

Impact/probability of occurrence reduced:

With synchronous mirror, the storage transaction is done to both instances simultaneously; each storage appliance confirms the storage transaction to the respective application instance and thus successful transaction is done after the data is stored on both instances. Thus, synchronous mirroring guarantees RPO near 0.

  1. M-2-1-05. Synchronous in-memory mirror

Fully Covered: R-2-05. Failure / outage of single storage controller (I), Failure / outage of single storage unit, all data lost (I), Failure outage of single storage unit, data loss (I)

Impact/probability of occurrence reduced:

In-memory computing is e.g. used to increase performance of database applications. To ensure high availability without introducing latency by storage transactions, synchronous mirroring is carried out in memory. Successful transactions are confirmed after the transaction is written to the storage on the primary side and replicated to the memory on the secondary side. The in-memory mirroring is controlled by the application. The LAN is used for data transfer.

  1. CG IS Architecture Framework Classes

This chapter introduces the most important components and features, summarizes the necessary resources and gives information about resources that can be shared with other systems.

9.2.1. Definition of Classes

9.2.1.1. Sizing

Several Classes are defined in the CG IS Architecture Framework. Two different size groups are distinguished:

  • Class 1 clusters are designed for small scale applications.

  • Larger clusters with a minimum of two hosts or two HCI appliances are classified as Class 2 - 4 Clusters. Scaling of compute and storage capacity by adding additional physical servers, HCI nodes and/or storage capacity is intended in the design.

  • Class 4 clusters can be implemented in two different flavors. Class 4a is implemented using a secondary storage. Class 4b consists of two independent pairs of Class 3 Clusters, which are synchronized by means of application or database mirror.

Class 3 and 4 stacks are certified to host Continuity Critical applications.

9.2.1.2. Compute Availability

For all classes, resources are provisioned sufficiently to guarantee quick recovery from a single host or HCI node outage. Classes 3 and 4 demand adequate capacities for continuous operation, even in the case of a full data center failure. Note that all of the components within a stack must provide the availability demands.

9.2.1.3. Data Availability

Concerning the data availability after a failure of storage components, several forms of storage provisioning are distinguished. The classes are distinguished by their demands for data availability. Hence, RPO for applications ranges from last backup with Class 1 to the support for Continuous Data Protection (CDP) with Class 4a and Class 4b. Higher classes implement all the mechanisms provided by the lower classes, e.g.:

  • Class 1: Data recovery after storage failure, i.e. backup is mandatory.

  • Class 2: Continuous operation after failure of single storage, i.e. mirror of data.

  • Classes 3 and 4: Continuous operation after the outage of an entire data center site, i.e. mirror to sister site.

  • Classes 3 and 4: Small RTOs, i.e. data protection is available for Class 4. For Class 3 the connection of backup devices via a high-speed network is implemented.

  • Class 4: Continuous Data Protection (CDP) must be implemented. However, CDP can be implemented on a per application basis.

  • Class 4a: Immediate restart and recovery of VMs in case of any failure of the primary storage. The storage configuration on a Class 4a stack supports instant recovery of data, which could be realized by secondary storage according to Figure 26 : Restart from secondary storage for Class 4a stacks (left) compared to Class 4b stacks (right).

  • Class 4b: Application-based mirroring is possible since a redundant, separate stack is provided. If mechanisms like application-based mirroring are applied, the failure of one primary storage will not disrupt operation of VMs using the other storage.

  1. Figure 26 : Restart from secondary storage for Class 4a stacks (left) compared to Class 4b stacks (right)

The minimum achievable RTO depends on the application that is restored and the technology of the hardware stack. The available bandwidth of the LAN and SAN, the I/O-performance of the storage and backup system, the amount of data and the scope of recovery determine the total recovery time. In a worst-case scenario, corrupted data is mirrored to the secondary site and a database needs to be fully reconstructed from backup and transaction/archive logs. Generally, the technical preconditions for fast recovery are best for Class 4 clusters since they provide secondary storage and a standby instance which can be used for application reboot.

9.2.1.4. VM Placement

The interoperability and availability of VMs hosting CC applications is of special importance. This implies rules for the placement of VMs on clusters hosting CC applications in order to optimize their performance.

9.2.1.5. Network Design

To ensure continuous operation of the hardware stacks network bottlenecks should be avoided. Accordingly, the bandwidth of inter-switch links must be high enough to avoid congestion. Therefore, continuous capacity management is strongly recommended.

For any class, the storage traffic needs to be separated from the LAN. In Class 1 clusters this is carried out at least logically. In Class 2 – 4 clusters the storage network, i.e. FC-based SAN as well as IP-based LAN, must physically be separated in any case.

For any class, connectivity of server hardware to the network switches must be implemented via redundant physical Ethernet interfaces. Additionally, the vMotion traffic must be routed via a dedicated network connection in Class 2 – 4 clusters.

As long as the limits specified above will not be exceeded, it is possible to share network switches with systems that are not certified within the CG IS Architecture Framework. This applies as well to converged switches used in certain hardware stacks

9.2.2. Storage Design within Classes

Storage types are related to the criticality of the applications and the size of the clusters:

  • Class 1 stacks may be equipped with storage systems connected to the commonly used network. Only Class 1 clusters can be set up without mirrored storage systems.

  • Class 2 - 4 stacks must be equipped with storage connected via a dedicated storage network, i.e. Fibre Channel network or IP-based network.

The choice of storage design also depends on the criticality of the application:

  • Only Class 1 stacks may be equipped with single storage.

  • In case of a Class 2 stack, synchronous storage mirroring is mandatory. This can either be done within a single site or optionally to a sister site.

  • The high criticality of applications running on Class 3 or 4 stacks demands for mirroring to a sister site. If technically feasible, the mirror must be synchronous. If technical reasons like high latencies do not allow synchronous mirroring to a sister site, the replication can be asynchronous for Class 3 stacks. In this case, a synchronous mirror at the primary site is mandatory. 9.2.3. Backup and data protection within Classes

The requirements for data availability directly influence the backup and data protection design of clusters:

  • Class 1 and 2 stacks are used to house data with a demand for regular availability. Thus, RTO is comparatively high. Since these clusters are based on single-site storage, backup media should be stored at a remote backup site to allow disaster recovery. Such backup sites could be another room at the location, another plant or even a Daimler regional data center.

  • The high criticality of data availability for applications on Class 3 and 4 stacks demands a short RTO. Therefore, storage devices or HCI clusters must feature integrated snapshot functionality and an interface to operating system and applications that ensures data consistency prior to snapshots. Snapshots are held on the local storage device to enable fast recovery in case of failure; additional backup media should be stored at a remote site to allow disaster recovery. With Class 4 stacks, backup is provided by remote secondary storage.

  • In a Class 3 and 4 stack any backup device must be connected at least via 8 Gigabit/s Fiber Channel or via 10 Gigabit/s Ethernet to allow fast restore.

Class 4 stacks provide Continuous Data Protection (CDP) for databases. Furthermore, CDP can be implemented per application and requires the application to support the technology. In any case, CDP is carried out by replicating data to a secondary storage system. Two methods for CDP are currently implemented:

  • CDP with almost zero RPO can be provided by the combination of frequent snapshots in combination with copies of archive logs (Figure 22 : Combination of periodic snapshots with copying of archive logs for continuous data protection.). In the case of databases, archive logs are transferred to secondary storage as soon as they have been written. This happens frequently (every minute in the case of CDP). Hence, with rollback to snapshots and subsequent application of transactions from archive logs any previous state may be restored until last archive log. Figure 30 : Database data in a Class 4a stack shows that there are two database VMs, one in each data center. Together with archive logs a rollback to earlier, consistent states is possible. To achieve (C)DP, the database writes transaction logs into files. Closed transaction logs are named Archive Logs. Since an open transaction log may not be accessible any more after a database failure, it cannot be used to restore data. Figure 31 : Recovery Points offered by a Class 4a stack summarizes the CDP capabilities of this method. With storage snapshots the frequency of generating recovery points is determined by the technology. From there, transactions from archive logs can be used to restore later states of the database. However, the granularity of available recovery points depends on the configured frequency of writing archive logs.
